5  Digital Libraries, Repositories, and Content Platforms

5.1 Chapter overview

A university purchases a repository platform and announces that it now has a digital library. Faculty upload PDFs, the archives scans photographs, and students build online exhibits. A year later, many records lack rights information, departments use inconsistent names, preservation copies are mixed with thumbnails, some files cannot be downloaded, and no one knows how to export the collection if the vendor contract ends.

The central problem of this chapter is how to turn selected digital content into a governed, discoverable, usable, and sustainable information service. A platform can store files and display pages, but it cannot decide what belongs in the collection, what an object represents, which metadata rules apply, whether consent permits online access, which copy should be preserved, or who will maintain the service after project funding ends.

Digital libraries, institutional repositories, digital archives, exhibits, collection management systems, and commercial content platforms overlap, but they are not synonyms. Their purposes, content models, workflows, user communities, rights responsibilities, and preservation commitments differ. This chapter compares those systems, follows a digital object from selection through ingest and access, and gives students a beginner framework for platform evaluation.

Chapter 4 established that metadata carries description, identity, rights, provenance, and relationships. Chapter 5 places that metadata inside operational workflows. The focus remains on service problems rather than software menus.

5.2 Learning objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. Differentiate digital libraries, institutional repositories, digital archives, digital exhibits, collection management systems, and commercial content platforms.
  2. Diagram a basic workflow from selection and rights review through ingest, quality assurance, publication, maintenance, and preservation.
  3. Distinguish master files, preservation copies, access derivatives, thumbnails, metadata records, and submission packages.
  4. Compare content platforms using metadata, interoperability, accessibility, security, preservation, governance, cost, and exit criteria.
  5. Create a small prototype collection with coherent metadata, identifiers, rights statements, and file relationships.
  6. Evaluate ethical and professional risks involving copyright, consent, privacy, cultural sensitivity, exclusion, takedown, and sustainability.

5.3 Key concepts

Table 5.1 supplies the working vocabulary. Several terms describe functions rather than specific products. A repository may support exhibits, and a digital library may use repository software, but their service commitments still need to be stated.

5.4 Core discussion

5.4.1 Different systems, different promises

The phrase digital library emphasizes organized collections and services: selection, description, discovery, access, use, and stewardship. It is broader than a website containing files. Users should be able to understand what the collection contains, find relevant objects, interpret them, and know how they may be used.

An institutional repository is typically tied to an organization’s intellectual output: theses, articles, reports, conference materials, datasets, learning objects, and other works. Lynch describes institutional repositories as organizationally defined services and infrastructure for managing and disseminating digital materials (Lynch 2003). The emphasis is not only software but institutional commitment.

A digital archive maintains digital records with attention to provenance, original order, authenticity, restrictions, and long-term context. A public interface may show item-level descriptions, but archival control can also operate at collection, series, file, or aggregate levels.

A digital exhibit selects objects to support an interpretive argument or story. It is curated and intentionally partial. Exhibit captions should link to fuller object records rather than replacing them.

A collection management system supports internal documentation and workflows such as accessioning, location control, conservation, loans, valuation, rights, and exhibition history. Its public portal may expose selected fields, but internal records can contain confidential or operational data.

A commercial content platform provides licensed discovery and delivery. The library may configure authentication, links, branding, and holdings, but it usually does not control source metadata, preservation, interface code, or long-term availability.

5.4.2 A digital object is more than one file

A digitized newspaper issue may include master images, cropped access images, OCR text, a searchable PDF, page-order metadata, descriptive metadata, checksums, rights statements, and links to the source volume. A born-digital thesis may include the submitted PDF, supplemental files, deposit agreement, embargo date, author identifier, department authority, and preservation events.

As Figure 5.1 indicates, a file without metadata may be unreadable in context, while metadata without a managed file cannot deliver the object. Relationships are especially important for compound objects, versions, translations, supplements, and collections.

5.4.3 Selection and collection policy

Digital projects often begin with opportunity: a grant, donated scanner, available platform, or anniversary. Sustainable services begin with a collection policy. The policy should define:

  • mission and intended communities;
  • subject, geographic, chronological, institutional, and format scope;
  • selection and exclusion criteria;
  • relationship to existing collections and mandates;
  • rights, consent, privacy, and cultural-sensitivity requirements;
  • descriptive depth and languages;
  • file formats and quality targets;
  • preservation commitment;
  • access levels and restrictions;
  • responsibilities for correction and takedown; and
  • resource limits and review schedule.

Selection is not neutral. Digitizing prominent collections first may reinforce an institution’s existing historical emphasis. Selection should consider significance, demand, condition, rights, representational gaps, community priorities, feasibility, and preservation risk.

5.4.4 Digitization and born-digital content

Digitization converts analog information into digital form. It does not automatically create a digital library or preserve the original. A scanning workflow should record the source object, equipment, settings, operator, date, processing, quality checks, file names, and relationships among derivatives.

A master file should retain sufficient quality for future use. An access derivative is optimized for current delivery. Thumbnails support browsing but should not become the only surviving copy. Format and quality choices depend on content, use, storage, standards, and institutional capacity.

OCR makes text in images searchable and reusable. It introduces errors, especially with degraded paper, complex layouts, historical type, diacritics, and Southeast Asian scripts. OCR should be stored as a derivative with software and quality provenance. It should not silently replace an authoritative transcription.

Born-digital content brings different risks: proprietary formats, external links, embedded media, passwords, macros, personal data, version ambiguity, and malware. Ingest should include inventory, validation, security scanning, rights review, and documentation of original context.

5.4.5 Ingest as a controlled workflow

Ingest is the controlled transfer of content into a managed system. It is not the same as dragging files into a folder. Figure 5.2 follows a basic lifecycle.

The feedback loop in Figure 5.2 matters. Publication is not the end. Files can corrupt, links break, rights change, communities request corrections, and platforms require upgrades. Every stage needs acceptance criteria and an accountable role.

A submission package may include:

  • content inventory;
  • source and derivative files;
  • descriptive, structural, technical, and rights metadata;
  • deposit, transfer, or consent agreement;
  • checksums;
  • virus-scan result;
  • restrictions and embargo dates;
  • identifiers and relationships; and
  • processing or exception notes.

Not every small project needs an elaborate package, but every project needs enough evidence to explain what was received, what was changed, what was accepted, and under what authority.

5.4.6 Content models and metadata

A content model defines how a platform represents communities, collections, items, files, people, versions, and relationships. DSpace, for example, supports repository structures and workflows documented across its communities, collections, items, bundles, and bitstreams (DSpace Project 2026). Omeka S centers resources, items, item sets, media, vocabularies, and sites for digital collections and exhibits (Omeka Team 2026).

These examples are not universal templates. Before configuring a platform, teams should map professional entities to platform entities. If one oral-history interview has audio, transcript, consent form, and photographs, will they be media attached to one item, separate related items, or a compound object? The answer affects metadata, rights, discovery, export, and preservation.

Metadata should follow an application profile, not whatever fields a default form happens to show. Required fields should reflect service needs. Controlled values should use authority sources. Administrative and restricted metadata should not be exposed accidentally through public APIs or exports.

5.4.7 Identifiers and stable access

A stable identifier separates identity from current location. Persistent identifier systems such as DOI and Handle depend on organizational maintenance, not magical strings. The DOI system, for example, combines identifiers with resolution and metadata obligations (International DOI Foundation 2026).

A repository should define:

  • what entity receives an identifier;
  • who assigns and maintains it;
  • whether it resolves publicly;
  • what happens after withdrawal;
  • how versions are distinguished; and
  • how identifiers appear in citations and exports.

Changing a platform should not force every citation to break. Redirects, identifier resolution, domain control, and export are part of continuity planning.

5.4.8 Discovery, harvesting, and interoperability

Repositories rarely serve users only through their own search box. Records may be discovered through search engines, union catalogues, subject portals, aggregators, ORCID profiles, citation links, and APIs.

OAI-PMH provides a protocol through which service providers can harvest metadata records from repositories using defined requests, identifiers, datestamps, sets, and metadata formats (Open Archives Initiative 2008). Harvesting increases reach but exposes metadata quality problems. If names, dates, rights, and types are inconsistent locally, aggregation magnifies inconsistency.

IIIF provides interoperable APIs for presenting and working with images and audiovisual resources. Its Presentation API describes the structure and layout of compound digital objects through manifests and related resources (IIIF Consortium 2024). IIIF can support deep zoom, page sequence, annotation, and reuse across viewers while leaving content under institutional control.

5.4.9 Access copies, restrictions, and embargoes

Open access is not identical to unrestricted access. Content may be limited by copyright, donor agreement, privacy, cultural protocol, security, research ethics, or temporary embargo. A platform should represent access decisions explicitly rather than using hidden files and informal notes.

Access levels might include open, registered-user, institutional, reading-room, mediated request, embargoed-until-date, and closed. Each level needs an authority, rationale, review date, and user-facing explanation. Metadata may remain public while files are restricted, but descriptive metadata can itself disclose sensitive information.

An embargo should expire through monitored workflow. A permanent “temporary” restriction is a governance failure. Takedown procedures should provide a contact, criteria, documentation, interim actions, review, decision, and appeal or correction route.

5.4.10 Rights and licenses

Owning a physical object does not automatically transfer copyright. Permission to digitize does not always include permission to publish globally, allow download, or authorize reuse. Deposit agreements should state rights granted, representations by depositors, embargoes, preservation permissions, permitted transformations, and takedown conditions.

Standard rights statements help users understand known conditions (RightsStatements.org 2026). A rights field should identify the statement, rights holder where known, source of determination, jurisdiction or date assumptions, and contact route. “For educational use only” is often too vague to guide lawful reuse.

5.4.11 Platform selection

Platform selection should begin with requirements and capacity. Table 5.4 presents criteria that apply to open-source, proprietary, locally hosted, and managed services. Open source can support inspectability and local control but still requires hosting and expertise. Proprietary hosting can reduce some operational burden but may increase dependency.

The crucial final row in Table 5.4 is often neglected. A screenshot of exported CSV is not enough. The team should verify that files, metadata, identifiers, relationships, rights, versions, and audit history can be reconstructed meaningfully.

5.4.12 Governance, sustainability, and community practice

A platform needs a governance group with authority over scope, priorities, metadata, rights, access, security, preservation, integrations, upgrades, and retirement. Roles may include collection owner, repository manager, metadata librarian, archivist, IT administrator, privacy or legal adviser, accessibility lead, and community representative.

The COAR Community Framework recommends good practices across discovery, access, reuse, integrity, quality assurance, preservation, sustainability, and governance (Confederation of Open Access Repositories 2023). The framework is useful because it treats repositories as community infrastructure rather than storage products.

Sustainability requires recurring resources: staff time, hosting, storage, backups, training, documentation, accessibility remediation, upgrades, identifiers, and user support. Grant funding can start a service but cannot substitute for an institutional owner.

Usage statistics should inform improvement without becoming the sole measure of value. Rare local-history resources may have modest traffic but high cultural importance. Metrics should document definitions, bot filtering, privacy controls, and limitations.

Reflective prompt. Choose a digital collection you have used. What promise does it appear to make about access, context, persistence, and correction? What evidence supports that judgment?

5.5 Philippine and Southeast Asian examples

5.5.1 State university repository: theses and embargoes

A Philippine state university requires electronic theses. The repository receives a PDF, adviser approval, abstract, department, keywords, ORCID where available, and an access decision. Some theses have patent, publication, or participant-confidentiality concerns. The workflow records an embargo end date and responsible office. Staff test expiry rather than relying on memory, and public metadata explains when the file will open.

5.5.2 Provincial library: digitized newspapers

A provincial library scans fragile newspapers. Each issue becomes a compound object with page order, master TIFF files, smaller JPEG images, OCR text, a searchable PDF, title and date metadata, source-volume identification, and rights notes. OCR quality is sampled because historical printing and damaged pages create errors. The public interface offers page images and text search, while preservation copies remain outside routine delivery.

5.5.3 Museum exhibit: object records behind the story

A museum creates an online exhibit about regional weaving. Exhibit pages combine narrative, interviews, photographs, and object details. Every displayed object links to a durable collection record with maker, materials, dimensions, provenance, community terminology, rights, and correction contact. When the exhibit ends, the object records remain managed rather than becoming orphaned media.

5.5.4 Community archive: tiered cultural access

A community archive holds public festival photographs, family histories, and recordings with culturally sensitive knowledge. It avoids a simple open/closed switch. Community-approved categories specify who may view files, which metadata may display, and when decisions must be reviewed. Platform configuration follows the protocol rather than defining it.

5.5.5 Regional aggregation: local records, shared discovery

Several Southeast Asian repositories expose selected Dublin Core records for harvesting. The aggregator normalizes type and language codes for filtering but preserves local titles, original scripts, identifiers, and source links. Mapping provenance allows users to distinguish the aggregator’s transformations from the contributing institution’s description.

5.6 Hands-on activity: Build a three-item prototype collection

5.6.1 Purpose

Design and document a small digital collection from selection through public access, including files, metadata, rights, quality review, and export.

5.6.2 Tools

Choose one:

  • Omeka S using an instructor-provided installation and its user documentation (Omeka Team 2026);
  • another approved repository sandbox; or
  • a local simulation using folders, CSV metadata, HTML item pages, and a manifest.

Also use a text editor, spreadsheet, checksum tool, and image editor that preserves the master file.

5.6.3 Source package

Use three fictional or instructor-cleared items from one coherent collection. Do not upload personal or copyrighted material without authorization. Suitable items include public-domain posters, institution-created photographs with permission, or fully fictional surrogates.

5.6.4 Steps

  1. Write a 150-word collection statement defining purpose, audience, scope, selection, exclusions, and stewardship commitment.
  2. Inventory the three source objects and state what entity each record describes.
  3. Create a master or submitted file and at least one access derivative where appropriate.
  4. Record file names, formats, sizes, checksums, source, and derivative relationships.
  5. Create item records using the Chapter 4 application profile. Include title, creator, date, type, format, identifier, source, language, subject, description, rights, and record provenance.
  6. Create one collection-level record and connect all items to it.
  7. Assign stable local identifiers and design public URLs that do not depend on file names.
  8. Add a rights statement, access condition, and takedown contact for every item.
  9. Configure or simulate browse, search, and one collection landing page.
  10. Test one mobile task, one keyboard-only task, and one known-item search with a peer.
  11. Export metadata and files. Verify whether relationships and identifiers can be reconstructed.
  12. Submit a change log and one prioritized improvement.

5.6.5 Expected output

Submit:

  • collection statement and selection rationale;
  • three item records and one collection record;
  • file inventory with checksums and derivative relationships;
  • rights, access, and takedown notes;
  • screenshots or local HTML showing collection and item views;
  • usability and accessibility test notes;
  • metadata and file export;
  • reconstruction or exit note; and
  • a 200-word reflection.

Reflection question. Which part of the prototype depended on software, which depended on metadata policy, and which depended on institutional or community authority?

5.7 Ethical and professional issue: access is not the same as permission

Digitization can increase access while increasing exposure. A photograph may be legally publishable but humiliating to depicted people. An oral-history narrator may have consented to research-room use, not global streaming. A sacred object may be widely photographed but governed by community protocols. A student thesis may include personal information that passed an academic defense but should not be indexed openly.

Ethical review should ask:

  • Who created, owns, appears in, speaks in, or is affected by the content?
  • What permission was actually granted, for which uses and audiences?
  • Does public metadata reveal sensitive facts even when the file is closed?
  • Can people request correction, restriction, contextualization, or takedown?
  • Who has authority to decide, and how are decisions recorded and reviewed?
  • Does the platform force a simpler access rule than the community requires?

Copyright review and ethical review overlap but are not identical. A public-domain object can still present privacy or cultural concerns. A copyrighted object may be lawfully available under permission, license, or an applicable exception. Rights metadata should communicate evidence and uncertainty rather than making unsupported claims.

Consent should be specific enough to guide workflows. A checkbox saying “I agree” may not explain search-engine indexing, download, reuse, automated transcription, AI processing, or cross-border hosting. Meaningful consent also requires a feasible way to revisit decisions where the agreement allows.

Takedown is not an admission that the project failed. It is part of accountable access. Institutions need a visible contact, interim response, documented review, preservation of evidence, and communication with affected parties. Removed public content may still require a tombstone record so citations and identifiers do not silently disappear.

Platform design can also exclude. Image-heavy exhibits may consume mobile data; complex viewers may fail with assistive technology; mandatory accounts may block casual inquiry; English-only interfaces may hide local-language collections. Access must be evaluated through actual community conditions.

Ethical checkpoint. A donor signed a broad deed in 1995, but people depicted in the photographs object to online facial recognition and unrestricted download. What legal evidence, community authority, technical options, and interim actions should guide the response?

5.8 Review questions

  1. Identification: Distinguish a digital library, institutional repository, digital archive, and digital exhibit.
  2. Identification: Define master file, preservation copy, access derivative, and thumbnail.
  3. Short answer: Why is ingest more than uploading files?
  4. Short answer: What information should accompany a submission package?
  5. Short answer: How do OAI-PMH and IIIF support different forms of interoperability?
  6. Applied analysis: A project stores only JPEG thumbnails and a spreadsheet. Identify the service and stewardship risks.
  7. Applied analysis: A repository migration exports metadata but loses file relationships and embargo dates. Which evaluation and exit tests were inadequate?
  8. Scenario: A thesis embargo has no end-date automation or responsible office. Propose a corrected workflow.
  9. Scenario: A digital exhibit receives a community takedown request. Outline an immediate and long-term response.
  10. Synthesis: Explain how collection policy, metadata, platform configuration, rights, and preservation jointly determine access.

5.9 Mini case: The centennial digital campus archive

Bayview College plans a centennial portal containing yearbooks, student newspapers, photographs, theses, oral histories, and memorabilia. The president wants everything searchable before the anniversary in six months. The vendor proposes one hosted platform with unlimited users but charges for large exports. Most yearbooks are under copyright; newspapers name students in disciplinary reports; oral-history consent forms mention classroom research but not public streaming. Scans are stored on one external drive, metadata use inconsistent names, and no preservation budget exists after the grant. Alumni want facial tagging, while the Indigenous student association asks for restrictions on ceremonial images.

Guide questions

  1. Divide the proposed content into appropriate system purposes, access categories, and phased priorities.
  2. Design an ingest and governance workflow naming decisions, evidence, roles, checks, identifiers, and review points.
  3. Create a platform evaluation and exit test that addresses files, metadata, relationships, rights, accessibility, costs, and community protocols.

5.10 Further reading and tools

5.10.1 Readings and frameworks

  • Lynch, “Institutional Repositories.” A foundational account of repositories as institutional infrastructure (Lynch 2003).
  • COAR Community Framework. Good practices for repository discovery, access, integrity, preservation, sustainability, and governance (Confederation of Open Access Repositories 2023).
  • OAI-PMH specification. Protocol for metadata harvesting (Open Archives Initiative 2008).
  • IIIF Presentation API. Interoperable structure and presentation of digital objects (IIIF Consortium 2024).
  • RightsStatements.org. Standardized rights statements for cultural heritage collections (RightsStatements.org 2026).
  • DOI Handbook. Identifier, metadata, and resolution responsibilities (International DOI Foundation 2026).

5.10.2 Open and widely accessible tools

  • Omeka S: digital collections, vocabularies, media, item sets, and exhibits (Omeka Team 2026).
  • DSpace: institutional repository software and documentation (DSpace Project 2026).
  • ArchivesSpace: archival description and collections management.
  • CollectiveAccess: collections management and public access.
  • OpenRefine: metadata cleanup and reconciliation before ingest.
  • ExifTool: extraction and inspection of embedded technical metadata.
  • BagIt tools: package files with manifests for transfer.
  • Fixity and checksum utilities: verify file integrity.
