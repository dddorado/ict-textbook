10  Digital Preservation and Cloud Services

10.1 Chapter overview

A provincial archive digitizes photographs and stores the master TIFF files on an external drive. Staff also upload JPEG copies to a cloud folder and describe the collection in a spreadsheet. Five years later, the drive still turns on, but several folders were renamed, the spreadsheet no longer matches the files, one cloud account belongs to a former employee, and no one has checked whether the copies are identical. The institution has storage and copies, yet it cannot demonstrate that the collection is complete, authentic, understandable, recoverable, or sustainable.

The central problem of this chapter is how to keep digital information understandable, trustworthy, findable, and usable for as long as it is needed despite technological, organizational, financial, legal, and environmental change. Digital preservation is not a one-time technical treatment. It is an ongoing service combining selection, policy, metadata, file management, storage, monitoring, security, rights, and planned action.

Preservation must be distinguished from digitization, storage, synchronization, and backup. Digitization creates digital representations. Storage holds bits. Synchronization makes locations reflect changes, including accidental deletion. Backup enables recovery from selected failures. Preservation coordinates these functions with identity, context, authenticity, access, and long-term responsibility.

Cloud services can contribute geographic separation, scalable storage, managed infrastructure, and automated controls. They do not transfer accountability away from the library or archive. Cost, connectivity, encryption, identity management, jurisdiction, service changes, data egress, environmental impact, and exit planning remain institutional concerns.

This chapter introduces digital objects, bitstreams, fixity, checksums, integrity, authenticity, redundancy, the 3-2-1 heuristic, file-format risk, migration, emulation, preservation metadata, OAIS information packages, storage tiers, cloud service and deployment models, service-level agreements, shared responsibility, vendor lock-in, and recovery testing.

10.2 Learning objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. Distinguish digitization, storage, synchronization, backup, disaster recovery, and digital preservation.
  2. Identify and assess technological, environmental, human, organizational, legal, and vendor threats to digital continuity.
  3. Generate, record, verify, and interpret SHA-256 checksums for a small preservation package.
  4. Propose a basic redundant-storage, monitoring, and recovery plan using the 3-2-1 heuristic critically.
  5. Compare preservation strategies and cloud options using format, cost, control, security, privacy, jurisdiction, sustainability, and exit criteria.
  6. Document preservation objects, events, agents, rights, and outcomes in a reviewable action plan.

10.4 Core discussion

10.4.1 Preservation is a managed commitment

Digital preservation begins with a mandate: what must remain available, for whom, for how long, and with which properties and restrictions? A university repository may preserve accepted theses indefinitely. A records center may retain records for legally defined periods and then destroy them through authorized disposition. A museum may preserve a complex interactive artwork whose behavior matters as much as its files.

Preserving everything forever is not a strategy. Selection, appraisal, retention, rights, and resource limits determine scope. The preservation policy should name responsible roles, designated communities, content types, minimum metadata, storage and monitoring expectations, access conditions, review cycles, and authority for actions.

The OAIS reference model describes an archive as an organization of people and systems that accepts responsibility for preserving information and making it available to a designated community (Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems 2012). OAIS is a conceptual model, not a software product or certificate.

10.4.3 Threats to digital continuity

Digital objects face interacting threats:

  • media degradation and hardware failure;
  • accidental deletion, overwriting, misfiling, or incomplete transfer;
  • malicious alteration, ransomware, account takeover, or insider abuse;
  • software, format, dependency, and interface obsolescence;
  • incomplete metadata or separation of files from context;
  • undocumented migration or transformation;
  • loss of keys, credentials, domains, subscriptions, or staff knowledge;
  • rights uncertainty and restrictions preventing preservation action;
  • vendor failure, contract termination, price change, or service redesign;
  • disasters, power instability, heat, humidity, flooding, fire, and political disruption;
  • institutional neglect, unclear ownership, and insufficient recurring funding.

A threat model connects assets, threats, vulnerabilities, consequences, and controls. “The cloud might fail” is too vague. A useful statement is: “A single administrator account controls deletion of all cloud copies; credential compromise could remove every version before detection.” Controls might include separate roles, multifactor authentication, immutable retention, alerts, delayed deletion, and an independently administered copy.

10.4.4 Files, bitstreams, and representation information

A file extension is a hint, not proof of format. A file named .pdf may be malformed or may contain embedded fonts, images, scripts, encryption, signatures, or attachments. Format identification examines signatures and structure. Validation asks whether the file conforms to a specification. Characterization records technical features needed for management.

A bitstream may be embedded within a file. A container such as a PDF, ZIP, video file, or disk image can contain multiple streams. Preserving bytes does not guarantee that future users can interpret them. Documentation, software dependencies, fonts, codecs, schemas, encryption keys, and contextual metadata may be necessary representation information.

10.4.5 Fixity and checksums

A checksum is calculated from a sequence of bytes. If one byte changes, a strong cryptographic hash such as SHA-256 will almost certainly produce a different value. Preservation systems record a checksum, later recalculate it, and compare the values.

Fixity checking can demonstrate that a bitstream has or has not changed since a recorded event. It cannot prove that the original file was correct, complete, authentic, virus-free, or meaningful. If a damaged file is ingested and hashed, later fixity checks can faithfully confirm the damaged bytes.

The openssl R package exposes SHA-256 hashing functions (Ooms 2026). A manifest should record algorithm, relative path, checksum, size, date, tool, and event outcome. Relative paths allow the package to move without rewriting every entry.

10.4.6 Authenticity requires more than fixity

Integrity concerns completeness and authorized change. Authenticity concerns whether an object is what it claims to be. Evidence may include provenance, chain of custody, identifiers, relationships, creator and transfer documentation, access controls, signatures, validation, and preservation events.

An authorized migration intentionally changes bytes. The new checksum differs, yet authenticity can be supported when the source and output, agent, tool, parameters, validation, date, reason, and relationship are documented. Conversely, an unchanged anonymous file with no provenance may have fixity but weak authenticity evidence.

10.4.7 Redundancy and the 3-2-1 heuristic

The 3-2-1 rule recommends at least three copies, on two kinds of storage, with one copy off-site. It is a useful beginner heuristic, not a guarantee. Three folders on one cloud account are not independent copies. Two media types in the same flood zone share a location risk. An offline disk that is never read may be unusable.

As Figure 10.1 shows, each copy participates in monitoring and recovery. Independence includes technology, location, credentials, administration, and failure mode. The NDSA Levels of Digital Preservation provide a staged framework across storage, integrity, control, metadata, and content (National Digital Stewardship Alliance 2019).

10.4.8 Inventory and identity

An organization cannot preserve what it does not know it holds. A preservation inventory records stable identifier, path or object key, size, format, dates, rights, source, responsible unit, retention, access restrictions, checksum, storage copies, and last check.

File names should be stable, unique within scope, and machine-safe, but filenames should not carry the only metadata. Renaming files can break links and authenticity evidence. If renaming is necessary, preserve original names and document the mapping.

Packaging formats can bind content and manifests for transfer. BagIt defines a hierarchical package with payload files and manifests (Kunze et al. 2018). A bag is not automatically a complete preservation repository; it supports reliable transfer and validation within a larger workflow.

10.4.9 File-format sustainability

No format is permanently safe. Format assessment considers:

  • public documentation and openness;
  • adoption and community support;
  • transparency versus compression or encryption;
  • self-documentation and embedded metadata;
  • external dependencies;
  • patent or licensing restrictions;
  • validation and rendering tools;
  • ability to preserve significant properties;
  • institutional capacity.

The Library of Congress sustainability guidance evaluates formats through factors such as disclosure, adoption, transparency, self-documentation, external dependencies, patents, and technical protection (Library of Congress 2026). Preferred-format lists should be reviewed, not treated as timeless rankings.

10.4.10 Significant properties and acceptable change

Migration cannot preserve every technical feature perfectly. Significant properties are characteristics judged necessary to retain meaning, evidential value, appearance, structure, or behavior for the designated community.

For a thesis, text, page order, figures, equations, citations, and accessibility may matter. For a spreadsheet, formulas, values, cell relationships, comments, formatting, and macros may have different significance. For an interactive artwork, timing, input, audiovisual behavior, and network dependencies may be central.

Significance is not purely technical. It follows mandate, user needs, records requirements, creator intent, cultural authority, and feasible resources. Document the judgment and validate migrations against it.

10.4.11 Preservation metadata and PREMIS

Preservation metadata records what an object is, what happened to it, who or what acted, which rights authorize action, and how components relate. PREMIS organizes preservation information around objects, events, agents, and rights (PREMIS Editorial Committee 2015).

10.4.12 OAIS packages and functions

OAIS uses three package concepts:

  • SIP: what a producer submits;
  • AIP: what the archive manages for preservation;
  • DIP: what the archive provides to a consumer.

The packages need not be one ZIP file. They are conceptual combinations of content information and preservation description information. Ingest validates and transforms submissions into managed archival packages. Access creates dissemination packages appropriate to user rights and needs.

Figure 10.2 highlights that preservation planning and administration continue after ingest. Deposit is the beginning of managed custody, not the end.

10.4.13 Cloud computing and service models

NIST defines cloud computing through on-demand access to shared configurable resources and identifies service and deployment models (Mell and Grance 2011). In simplified terms:

  • IaaS: the provider manages facilities, network, servers, and virtualization; the customer manages operating systems, applications, identities, configuration, and data.
  • PaaS: the provider also manages more of the application platform; the customer manages deployed applications, data, and access.
  • SaaS: the provider operates the application; the customer configures use, identities, data, workflows, and governance.

“Managed by provider” does not mean “no customer responsibility.” The institution must configure access, monitor use, classify data, maintain metadata, check exports, and understand contractual controls. Security and privacy responsibilities vary by service and agreement (Jansen and Grance 2011).

Cloud deployment can be public, private, community, or hybrid. The labels do not by themselves determine security or suitability. Architecture, configuration, operations, contracts, and evidence matter.

10.4.14 Storage tiers, durability, availability, and recovery

Cloud object storage often offers hot, cool, and archival tiers. Lower storage prices may bring retrieval delays, minimum retention periods, request charges, or egress fees. A preservation copy that takes hours to restore may be acceptable if another access copy exists, but incident plans must reflect that delay.

Durability estimates the likelihood that stored objects remain intact. Availability concerns whether they can be accessed when needed. Neither is a substitute for independent verification and recovery testing. Provider replication within one account may protect hardware failure but not customer deletion, compromised credentials, billing suspension, legal seizure, or provider-wide defects.

10.4.16 Service-level agreements and shared responsibility

An SLA should define measurable service scope, availability, support response, durability where applicable, incident notice, backup and restore, maintenance, data location, deletion, export, and remedies. A service credit after failure may not compensate for irreplaceable cultural loss.

A shared-responsibility matrix names each control and assigns provider, institution, or joint responsibility. Avoid vague statements such as “security is shared.” For example:

  • provider: physical data-center controls;
  • institution: classification and least-privilege roles;
  • joint: incident communication and evidence exchange;
  • institution: independent preservation metadata and off-provider copy.

10.4.17 Cost and total cost of stewardship

Preservation costs include selection, appraisal, rights, transfer, metadata, quality control, storage, monitoring, validation, security, access, staff training, format action, audits, incident response, migration, and exit. Cloud invoices add operations, retrieval, egress, API requests, monitoring, and support.

A cost model should state assumptions: annual growth, number and size of copies, access frequency, retention, geographic regions, retrieval tests, staffing, currency exposure, and price change. Comparing one local disk purchase with one month of cloud storage ignores staffing, replacement, power, facilities, risk, and exit.

10.4.18 Exit planning and vendor lock-in

An exit plan should exist before signing a contract. It identifies triggers, authority, export formats, APIs, metadata, checksums, logs, identities, encryption keys, bandwidth, egress cost, destination capacity, validation, parallel operation, final deletion, and proof.

A tabletop exit test asks: Can the institution list everything? Export a sample? Reconstruct relationships? Verify checksums? Estimate time and cost for all content? Continue access during transition? Confirm vendor deletion afterward?

If the answer depends on an employee’s memory or a proprietary interface, preservation risk is high.

10.5 Philippine and Southeast Asian examples

10.5.1 Provincial archive: typhoon-aware redundancy

A provincial archive stores digitized land and municipal records in a flood-prone area. One managed copy remains on-site for work, a second is held at a partner institution in another island group, and a third is stored in cloud object storage under a separately administered account. Quarterly fixity checks and annual restoration exercises are documented. The arrangement considers geographic hazards, connectivity, and the need for local access after disruption.

10.5.2 University repository: theses and embargoes

A university repository preserves submitted PDFs, supplemental files, deposit agreements, embargo dates, metadata, and preservation events. Public access copies are separate from restricted archival copies. Cloud storage rules do not accidentally publish embargoed files, and exit tests verify that rights and restriction metadata export with content.

10.5.3 Public library: community oral histories

A city library preserves uncompressed or losslessly compressed audio masters, interview agreements, transcripts, access derivatives, and community restrictions. Migration tests compare duration, channels, sampling characteristics, embedded metadata, and intelligibility. Consent is reviewed before cloud replication across jurisdictions.

10.5.4 Government records center: retention and authorized deletion

A government records center distinguishes preservation from permanent retention. Cloud immutability protects records during their retention period, but authorized disposition remains possible after legal, audit, and archival requirements are satisfied. Deletion includes replicas and provider backups according to contract, with evidence recorded.

10.5.5 Regional museum consortium: shared capacity without shared passwords

Several Southeast Asian museums pool preservation storage. Each institution retains identifiers, metadata, and authority over its collections. Federated identity and role-based access replace shared administrator passwords. Agreements define incident response, cost allocation, withdrawal, community restrictions, and recovery when one partner loses connectivity.

Reflect: Which disaster is most visible in your institution’s planning, and which slower organizational or technical threat is easier to overlook?

10.6 Hands-on activity: Build and test a small preservation package

10.6.1 Purpose

Create a preservation package for three to five non-sensitive files, record inventory and SHA-256 fixity, maintain three controlled copies, simulate unintended change, restore from a verified copy, and document the event. This activity contributes to the ethics and preservation brief.

10.6.2 Tools

  • R and Quarto;
  • R package openssl (Ooms 2026);
  • three to five instructor-provided or self-created non-sensitive files in at least two formats;
  • three storage locations approved for class use, with at least one logically or geographically separate;
  • the package fields in Table 10.6.

Table 10.6 combines technical and stewardship evidence. Do not place passwords, personal data, or sensitive locations in the exercise package.

10.6.3 Steps

  1. State the collection purpose, designated users, retention expectation, significant properties, rights, and excluded sensitive content.
  2. Create package/data/, package/metadata/, and package/logs/. Copy the selected source files into data/; do not alter the originals.
  3. Inventory each file with stable object ID, relative path, original filename, size, format, source, rights, and significant properties.
  4. Calculate SHA-256 checksums in R. Export metadata/manifest-sha256.csv and record tool and date.
  5. Verify every file immediately against the manifest. Record a FIXITY_CHECK event with PASS or FAIL.
  6. Create three named copies using two storage types, with one separate from the primary location. Record location, administrator, encryption, access, and sync behavior.
  7. Verify all three copies independently. Do not assume the copy command succeeded.
  8. On a disposable test copy only, change one byte or edit one file. Recalculate checksums and observe the failure.
  9. Quarantine the altered copy. Restore the affected file from an independently verified copy. Recalculate fixity and record investigation, restoration, agent, time, and outcome.
  10. Select one format and complete a risk note covering documentation, adoption, dependencies, validation tools, and significant properties.
  11. Draw a storage and responsibility diagram showing copies, failure domains, administrators, monitoring, and recovery path.
  12. Compare one local and one cloud option using the criteria in Table 10.5. Estimate storage and exit assumptions rather than quoting only a headline price.
  13. Write a one-page preservation action plan with schedule, owners, incident steps, annual restore test, format review, and exit trigger.
  14. Render the Quarto report and verify that another student can reconstruct what happened without oral explanation.

10.6.4 Expected output

Submit:

  • preservation package with data/, metadata/, and logs/;
  • inventory and SHA-256 manifest;
  • fixity log showing initial checks, deliberate failure, quarantine, restore, and final pass;
  • storage and responsibility diagram;
  • format-risk and significant-properties note;
  • local-versus-cloud comparison;
  • one-page preservation action plan; and
  • a 200-word reflection: Which evidence demonstrates continuity, and which preservation claims remain assumptions?

10.7 Ethical and professional issue: preservation rights, privacy, and sustainable custody

Preserving a digital object can conflict with access restrictions, privacy, copyright, community authority, and the right to be forgotten or to restrict harmful exposure. Copying, migration, decryption, format analysis, and cloud replication are preservation actions, but technical necessity does not automatically provide legal or ethical authority.

Archives may hold records containing personal addresses, health information, political activity, disciplinary records, or names of vulnerable people. Cultural collections may contain sacred or restricted knowledge. Cloud replication can move data into additional jurisdictions and expose it to provider personnel or legal demands. Encryption protects some risks but complicates key preservation; losing the key can make intact copies permanently inaccessible.

Responsible stewardship includes:

  • document authority for preservation copying, transformation, and access;
  • separate preservation retention from public availability;
  • classify sensitive content and apply proportionate access controls;
  • minimize cloud metadata that can reveal identities or collection sensitivity;
  • record embargoes, community protocols, takedown, and review dates;
  • maintain key custody, recovery, rotation, and authorized destruction;
  • obtain deletion evidence when retention authority ends;
  • assess vendor location, subcontractors, incident reporting, and data reuse;
  • budget for ongoing custody rather than launching unfunded collections;
  • consult affected communities about significant properties and acceptable access.

Under the Philippine Data Privacy Act, long-term retention of personal information still requires lawful purpose, proportionality, security, and retention discipline (Republic of the Philippines 2012; National Privacy Commission 2023). “For preservation” should not become an unlimited exemption from governance.

Environmental sustainability is also professional. Multiple copies, computation, media replacement, and cloud storage consume energy and materials. The response is not to abandon redundancy, but to appraise responsibly, avoid uncontrolled duplication, choose appropriate storage tiers, monitor actual use, and include environmental cost in stewardship decisions.

Professional judgment prompt: A donor gives a hard drive of community photographs with no agreements and asks the archive to “keep everything forever in the cloud.” What must be clarified before copying, identifying, uploading, or providing access?

10.8 Review questions

  1. Identification: Distinguish storage, synchronization, backup, disaster recovery, and digital preservation.
  2. Identification: What does a checksum establish, and what can it not establish?
  3. Short answer: Why are three folders in one cloud account not three independent preservation copies?
  4. Short answer: Explain the relationship among integrity, authenticity, provenance, and fixity.
  5. Applied analysis: A file fails a SHA-256 check. List the immediate actions that should occur before replacing anything.
  6. Applied strategy: Compare migration and emulation for an interactive digital exhibit. Which significant properties might drive the choice?
  7. Scenario: A cloud archive tier is inexpensive to store but costly and slow to retrieve. Which recovery, cost, and exit questions must be answered?
  8. Scenario: A repository exports files but not embargoes, relationships, or event logs. Why is the exit incomplete?
  9. Scenario: An institution keeps encrypted preservation copies but only one employee knows the key password. Identify the risks and controls.
  10. Critical analysis: When can authorized deletion be part of responsible digital stewardship rather than preservation failure?

10.9 Mini case: The cloud repository renewal

Luntian State University stores 60 terabytes of theses, research data, and digitized archives in a vendor-hosted repository. The renewal price will double. The vendor reports “eleven nines” of durability but does not expose object checksums. Export is available as files and Dublin Core CSV, but compound-object relationships, access restrictions, version history, and preservation events require a custom paid service. Egress is charged per gigabyte. Two administrators share one account, no restore exercise has occurred, and some donor agreements prohibit storage outside the Philippines. The university has ninety days to renew or migrate.

10.9.1 Guide questions

  1. Which technical, metadata, security, rights, jurisdiction, financial, and governance risks should be addressed first?
  2. What evidence and sample exports are needed to determine whether a complete, authentic migration is possible?
  3. Propose a ninety-day triage plan and a longer-term architecture that reduces future lock-in without pretending all risk can be eliminated.

10.10 Further reading and tools

10.10.1 Readings and standards

  • OAIS Reference Model. Foundational concepts for archival information packages, functions, and designated communities (Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems 2012).
  • PREMIS Data Dictionary. Preservation metadata for objects, events, agents, and rights (PREMIS Editorial Committee 2015).
  • NDSA Levels of Digital Preservation. A staged assessment and planning framework (National Digital Stewardship Alliance 2019).
  • Library of Congress Sustainability of Digital Formats. Format descriptions and sustainability factors (Library of Congress 2026).
  • Digital Preservation Handbook. Accessible guidance across organizational and technical preservation practice (Digital Preservation Coalition 2026).
  • NIST cloud definition and security guidance. Core service models plus public-cloud security and privacy considerations (Mell and Grance 2011; Jansen and Grance 2011).
  • BagIt RFC 8493. A standardized packaging format for transferring files with manifests (Kunze et al. 2018).

10.10.2 Open or widely accessible tools

  • R openssl package: calculate SHA-256 checksums in reproducible workflows (Ooms 2026).
  • PowerShell Get-FileHash or platform SHA tools: verify checksums independently of R.
  • DROID or Siegfried: identify file formats using maintained signature data.
  • JHOVE: identify, validate, and characterize selected formats.
  • BagIt tools: create and validate transfer packages; a valid bag is only one component of preservation.
  • Archivematica: open-source preservation workflow system useful for studying ingest, normalization, metadata, and packages.
  • Fixity and inventory scripts: small tools are valuable when their source, versions, logs, schedules, and failure handling are documented.

10.10.3 Closing synthesis

Digital preservation is the managed continuity of information, not the indefinite survival of one disk or vendor account. Checksums provide evidence of bit-level change. Redundant copies reduce selected failure risks. Metadata preserves identity, provenance, rights, events, and relationships. Format strategies address obsolescence. Recovery tests demonstrate that copies can become service again.

Cloud services can strengthen preservation architecture, but they introduce new dependencies, costs, jurisdictions, and exit problems. The professional question is therefore not “local or cloud?” It is: Which risks are controlled, which are transferred or concentrated, what evidence demonstrates continuity, who remains accountable, and can the institution recover or leave? Chapter 11 builds on this risk-based reasoning by integrating cybersecurity, law, ethics, and project planning.
