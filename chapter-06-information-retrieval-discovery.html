6  Information Retrieval and Discovery Systems

6.1 Chapter overview

A first-year student types climate change Philippines into a university discovery box. The system returns tens of thousands of records. A recent local policy report appears below older foreign articles; several promising records have no full-text link; a digitized map in the university repository does not appear at all. The search has produced results, but has it supported discovery?

The central problem of this chapter is how an information system turns a human information need into a useful, explainable, and actionable set of results. People ask questions in incomplete and changing language. Systems work with stored representations: terms, fields, identifiers, relationships, holdings, licenses, and usage signals. Information retrieval (IR) joins these worlds through indexing, query processing, matching, ranking, display, and access services.

Retrieval is not merely a search-box feature. It is an infrastructure connecting cataloguing, metadata, databases, repositories, licensed collections, archives, reference work, and user interfaces. A ranking algorithm cannot recover a subject term that was never assigned. A beautiful interface cannot supply a missing license link. A complete index may still fail a learner who does not know the vocabulary of a field. Discovery quality is therefore a shared responsibility among metadata workers, systems librarians, collection managers, vendors, reference librarians, and users.

This chapter explains the retrieval pipeline, Boolean and fielded searching, text processing, relevance ranking, facets, precision and recall, discovery layers, federated search, and link resolution. It also asks whose language, collections, and behavior become visible in a discovery system. The aim is not to memorize one vendor interface, but to develop concepts that transfer across catalogues, databases, repositories, archives, and web-scale discovery services.

6.2 Learning objectives

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. Explain how records, fields, tokens, indexes, queries, matching, and ranking support retrieval.
  2. Construct Boolean, phrase, fielded, truncated, and faceted searches for a stated information need.
  3. Calculate and interpret precision and recall in a small relevance-judgment set.
  4. Compare catalogues, subject databases, repositories, federated search tools, and discovery layers by coverage and function.
  5. Diagnose retrieval failures involving metadata, indexing, query processing, ranking, interface design, or access resolution.
  6. Recommend an ethical and evidence-based improvement to a discovery service.

6.4 Core discussion

6.4.1 From an information need to an accessible item

An information need is not identical to the words typed into a box. A student who enters rice pest may need a recent, locally applicable treatment guide written in Filipino and available without payment. The two-word query expresses only part of that need. During searching, the student may learn the scientific name of a pest, discover a government agency, or decide that date matters more than language. Searching is therefore iterative: the user and the system exchange signals, and the user’s understanding changes.

Figure 6.1 shows the service cycle. The upper path describes system work; the lower return path shows how results help a person revise the query or even the original question.

As Figure 6.1 indicates, successful discovery has at least two outcomes: the user identifies a suitable resource and can take a meaningful next action. A record marked “available” that leads to a broken link is a discovery failure even if the ranking was excellent.

6.4.2 Records, fields, and the searchable corpus

A corpus is the body of records or content that a system can search. In a catalogue it may consist mainly of bibliographic and holdings records. In a repository it may include metadata, abstracts, OCR text, and full text. In an archival system, collection- and series-level descriptions may be more prominent than item records. A discovery layer may combine a local catalogue, repository records, electronic-resource metadata, open-access sources, and a large vendor index.

Coverage determines what can possibly be found. A resource outside the corpus has zero chance of retrieval, regardless of the query. Coverage may be limited by collection decisions, licenses, harvesting failures, incompatible metadata, embargoes, or deliberate restrictions. NISO’s Open Discovery Initiative emphasizes transparency among content providers and discovery-service providers because coverage and indexing arrangements affect what users see (National Information Standards Organization 2020).

Structured records divide description into fields. Title, creator, subject, date, format, language, identifier, abstract, and full text can be indexed differently. A title field may receive a high ranking weight; a date field may support ranges; an identifier may require exact matching; a subject field may preserve controlled terms. This is one reason metadata remains central to discovery. When the title Mga Kuwentong Bayan ng Panay is placed in an undifferentiated note field, title searching and display may both fail.

6.4.3 How an inverted index works

Scanning every document for every query would be slow at library scale. Retrieval systems usually prepare an index in advance. A simplified pipeline normalizes text, divides it into tokens, and records where those tokens occur. An inverted index points from a term to documents, reversing the document-to-words relationship familiar to readers (Manning et al. 2008).

Stemming can increase recall but may join unrelated forms; controlled vocabulary can improve consistency but requires governance and may not reflect community language.

Real indexes may store term positions for phrase searching, field identifiers for fielded search, frequency counts for ranking, language information, and permissions for access control. Indexing is not a one-time task. New and changed records must be added, deleted records removed, and mappings checked when schemas change.

6.4.4 Query formulation: translating a question

Good searching begins by separating concepts rather than typing a full assignment sentence. Consider: “How do public libraries support disaster preparedness in coastal communities?” The main concepts might be public libraries, disaster preparedness, and coastal communities. Each concept can have alternative terms:

("public librar*" OR "community librar*")
AND ("disaster preparedness" OR "disaster risk reduction" OR resilience)
AND (coastal OR island OR maritime)

This representation uses several common techniques:

  • AND requires both concepts and usually narrows a result set.
  • OR accepts alternatives and usually broadens a result set.
  • NOT excludes matches but can remove useful records and should be used cautiously.
  • Phrase searching requests adjacent words in order, although exact syntax varies.
  • Truncation such as librar* may retrieve library, libraries, and librarianship; the symbol and behavior vary by system.
  • Fielded searching limits a condition to a field, such as title, author, subject, or date.
  • Facets narrow a retrieved set by values such as format, year, language, location, or access status.

Table 6.3 connects each technique to a likely service problem. Interface labels and syntax must always be checked because systems implement these features differently.

As Table 6.3 suggests, query formulation and metadata quality are inseparable. A language facet cannot reliably narrow results if language codes are missing. An author search depends on names being placed in creator fields and, ideally, connected through authority control.

6.4.5 Matching and relevance ranking

Boolean retrieval asks whether a record satisfies stated conditions. Ranked retrieval asks how strongly each record matches. Many systems combine both: filters impose conditions, while a scoring model orders the matches.

Ranking can consider term frequency, rarity across the corpus, field weights, phrase proximity, date, document type, popularity, citation signals, availability, and configured boosts. BM25, a widely used probabilistic ranking family, increases a score when query terms occur meaningfully in a document while moderating the effects of document length and repeated occurrences (Robertson and Zaragoza 2009). Apache Lucene, used within many search applications, provides a BM25 similarity implementation (Apache Lucene Project 2025). Students do not need to derive the formula in an introductory course, but they should understand three points:

  1. A relevance score is calculated, not discovered as an objective property.
  2. Different configurations can rank the same records differently.
  3. High-ranked results reflect indexed evidence and design choices, not the full value of a work.

Relevance is contextual. A 2005 global review may be topically relevant to typhoon preparedness but unsuitable for a student who needs current Philippine local-government procedures. A primary source may be valuable to a historian even when it mentions the search terms only once. Systems approximate relevance; users and professionals interpret it.

6.4.6 Metadata as retrieval infrastructure

Full-text searching is powerful, but it does not make metadata obsolete. Metadata supplies distinctions that text alone often cannot:

  • a person’s role as author, editor, translator, or subject;
  • an authorized name across spelling and name changes;
  • a work’s language, format, date, audience, and rights status;
  • the relationship among an original, translation, edition, adaptation, and digitized copy;
  • the hierarchy of an archive or the sequence of a compound object;
  • local holdings, shelf location, license conditions, and access restrictions.

Controlled vocabularies connect records through consistent concepts, while user keywords capture current and local language. Effective discovery often uses both. A system may map a user’s term to an authorized heading, search notes and OCR text for additional recall, and display the authorized label as a facet. Metadata quality dimensions such as completeness, accuracy, consistency, and conformance directly influence discovery (Park 2009).

Crosswalking records from many sources introduces loss. A detailed local field may be mapped to a broad central field; names may arrive as uncontrolled strings; dates may use incompatible formats; subject headings may be combined with keywords. The interface may appear unified while the underlying evidence remains uneven. Discovery teams should therefore examine field mappings, not only the search screen.

6.4.7 Precision and recall

Retrieval evaluation requires a stated need and judgments about which items are relevant. Four counts organize the basic evaluation:

  • true positives (TP): relevant items retrieved;
  • false positives (FP): nonrelevant items retrieved;
  • false negatives (FN): relevant items not retrieved;
  • true negatives (TN): nonrelevant items not retrieved.

Precision and recall are then:

\[ \text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP+FP} \]

\[ \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP+FN} \]

A systematic search for evidence or a legal records request may favor recall because missing a relevant item has serious consequences. Walters argues that relevance measures can complement use statistics in library assessment by focusing attention on what searches retrieve and miss (Walters 2016).

Precision and recall also have limits. Relevance judgments can differ between people and change over time. A binary relevant/not relevant label hides degrees of usefulness. Testing only popular English queries can conceal failures for Filipino, Cebuano, Bahasa Indonesia, Thai, or community terminology. Evaluation should combine measures with task observation, query-log analysis under privacy controls, interviews, accessibility testing, and metadata review.

6.4.8 Facets and exploratory discovery

A facet is not simply a filter displayed on the left side of a page. It is a structured view of values present in the result set. Format, topic, creator, date, language, collection, location, and access status can help users understand a corpus while narrowing it.

Useful facets require coherent metadata and understandable labels. A format facet containing Text, Book, eBook, Electronic resource, and PDF may mix content type, carrier, and file format. A geographic facet that lists both Philippines and Philippine Islands may split related records. Counts can mislead when records have multiple values or when metadata coverage is incomplete.

Facet design should follow user tasks. An archival researcher may need collection, record group, date, place, and creator. A school learner may understand Grade level better than a technical audience code. A government information service may need agency, issuance type, jurisdiction, and effectivity date. Good facets expose meaningful structure without demanding that users understand the database schema.

6.4.9 Catalogues, databases, repositories, and discovery layers

Different systems answer different questions. Table 6.5 compares common services.

As Table 6.5 shows, no system is universally complete. A discovery layer offers a useful starting point but does not replace specialized databases, archival context, or professional search strategy. Teaching should avoid both extremes: presenting the single search box as comprehensive, or treating it as academically inferior. Its fitness depends on the task.

A central-index discovery service searches a preharvested index, which can be fast and consistently ranked. Federated search sends the query to remote sources at search time. Federated results may reflect current remote data, but differences in syntax, response speed, ranking, and record structure make merging difficult. The technical distinction matters when diagnosing missing records: did harvesting fail, did a remote target time out, or did field translation change the query?

6.4.10 From citation to copy: link resolution

Discovery often identifies a citation before it identifies an accessible copy. A link resolver receives metadata about the cited item plus institutional context, checks a knowledge base of holdings and licenses, and offers appropriate services. OpenURL standardizes a framework for carrying this context (The OpenURL Framework for Context-Sensitive Services 2010). A resolver may link to licensed full text, an open copy, the catalogue, document delivery, or interlibrary loan.

Older and specialized interoperability protocols remain relevant. Z39.50 supports search and retrieval across systems and continues to appear in library environments (Library of Congress 2026). OAI-PMH supports metadata harvesting for repositories (Open Archives Initiative 2008). These protocols do different jobs: one should not be described as a replacement for the other.

6.4.11 Diagnosing retrieval as a layered service

When users report “search is broken,” a useful diagnosis moves through layers:

  1. Coverage: Is the source or record in the searchable corpus?
  2. Metadata: Are essential values present, accurate, and mapped to the expected fields?
  3. Indexing: Was the record processed, tokenized, and updated correctly?
  4. Query: Did syntax, language processing, spelling correction, or field translation alter the request?
  5. Matching and ranking: Was the record matched but placed too low? Which boosts or filters mattered?
  6. Interface: Could the user interpret results, facets, availability, and system scope?
  7. Delivery: Did authentication, link resolution, rights, or holdings prevent access?
  8. Evaluation: Is the reported failure reproducible for a stated task and user context?

This layered method prevents expensive but irrelevant responses. Replacing the interface will not repair missing repository harvesting; changing the ranking model will not correct a broken subscription date range.

6.5 Philippine and Southeast Asian examples

6.5.1 Academic library: one query, several evidence environments

A Philippine university student researching jeepney modernization might begin with a discovery layer, continue to a transport or policy database, search the institutional repository for local theses, and consult government websites for current issuances. The discovery layer offers breadth, but a local repository may contain the most contextually relevant work. Librarians can teach a search pathway rather than a preferred box: identify the evidence type, choose a likely corpus, translate concepts, judge results, and follow access options.

6.5.2 Public library: multilingual local-history discovery

A city library digitizes photographs labelled in English, Filipino, and a local language. Community members search former street names, nicknames, barangay names, and terms used before administrative boundary changes. The library can retain original captions, add current and historical names, record language, link place authorities, and invite documented community corrections. This approach improves retrieval while preserving provenance instead of silently replacing older description.

6.5.3 School library: age-appropriate facets

A school catalogue has subject headings suitable for professional control but difficult for younger pupils. The library retains the controlled headings for consistency and adds a student-facing topic facet with tested plain-language labels. Search logs are reviewed only in aggregate and with a short retention period. The design connects knowledge organization to age-appropriate UX and privacy.

6.5.4 Government information service: version and authority

A public information portal contains memoranda, circulars, forms, and superseded issuances. Keyword relevance alone can place an obsolete document first. Structured fields for issuing agency, issuance type, number, effectivity date, amendment relationship, and current status allow the interface to label superseded material and prioritize authoritative versions without erasing the historical record.

6.5.5 Regional cultural heritage aggregation

A Southeast Asian cultural heritage portal may aggregate records produced under different cataloguing traditions, scripts, languages, and levels of description. A single “creator” field can flatten distinctions among artist, collector, photographer, community, and donor. Crosswalks should document loss, interfaces should expose source institutions, and search evaluation should include local scripts and transliterations. Aggregation expands reach, but it does not remove the need to respect source context.

Reflect: Choose a local topic whose name has changed over time. What contemporary term, historical term, variant spelling, language form, broader term, and narrower term would you include in a test query set?

6.6 Hands-on activity: Compare three discovery environments

6.6.1 Purpose

Investigate how corpus coverage, metadata, search features, ranking, and access services affect results for one information need. This lab contributes to the short-lab portfolio and prepares the UX or discovery critique.

6.6.2 Tools

  • a spreadsheet application or R with Quarto;
  • access to three systems, preferably a library catalogue or discovery layer, a subject database or national portal, and an institutional repository;
  • browser privacy mode if testing personalization is relevant;
  • the search-log fields in Table 6.6.

Table 6.6 makes the procedure reproducible. Do not record usernames, patron identifiers, or sensitive personal topics in a shared file.

6.6.3 Steps

  1. Write one information-need statement. Include at least two contextual requirements, such as Philippine coverage, publication date, language, audience, evidence type, or online availability.
  2. Identify two or three main concepts. List synonyms, variant spellings, controlled terms, abbreviations, and local-language terms.
  3. Select three discovery environments with meaningfully different corpora.
  4. Run the same basic query in each system. Record the exact query, date, claimed scope, result count, available facets, and first ten results.
  5. Judge each of the first ten results as relevant, partly relevant, or not relevant. State a reason based on the information need, not only the presence of keywords.
  6. For a simple comparison, count “relevant” as relevant and compute precision at 10: relevant items in the first ten divided by ten. Do not call this full-system recall; the total number of relevant items is unknown.
  7. Revise the search once in each system using a phrase, field, Boolean operator, controlled term, or facet. Record what changed.
  8. Attempt to access the two most promising items from each system. Record whether the link opened, required authentication, led to a request service, or failed.
  9. Classify one important problem per system as coverage, metadata, indexing/query processing, ranking, interface, or delivery.
  10. Recommend one feasible improvement and identify who would need to act: metadata staff, systems staff, reference staff, vendor, content provider, or policy owner.

6.6.4 Expected output

Submit:

  • a completed search log for three systems;
  • a concept-and-synonym map;
  • a table comparing precision at 10 before and after one revision;
  • two annotated screenshots with personal data removed;
  • a 300-word recommendation connecting evidence to one service improvement; and
  • a 100-word reflection answering: What did the system make easy to see, and what did it make easy to overlook?

6.7 Ethical and professional issue: ranking, privacy, and representational harm

Discovery systems distribute attention. A result on the first page is more likely to be read, cited, purchased, or remembered than a result on page twenty. Ranking therefore has professional consequences even when no person intends discrimination.

Bias can enter through several routes. Collections reflect historical funding and exclusion. Metadata may use outdated, colonial, stigmatizing, or externally imposed language. Full-text availability can boost well-funded publishers over local print sources. Usage-based ranking can amplify what already receives attention. English-language test queries can make other languages appear less relevant. Vendor coverage agreements may be invisible to users. Automated query expansion, recommendation, and generative summaries can add further opacity.

The professional response is not to promise a neutral system. It is to establish accountable discovery:

  • document corpus scope, important exclusions, and update schedules;
  • maintain provenance when description is corrected or supplemented;
  • test named user tasks across languages, devices, and access conditions;
  • include affected communities in terminology and interface decisions;
  • distinguish organic relevance, configured boosts, sponsored placement, and recommendations;
  • provide routes for reporting harmful description and broken access;
  • retain query logs only when necessary, minimize identifiers, restrict access, and set deletion rules;
  • require vendor explanations of coverage, ranking controls, analytics, and data reuse;
  • keep human review for high-impact automated changes.

Search logs deserve special care. They can expose health concerns, political interests, religious questions, identities, locations, and research plans. The Philippine Data Privacy Act requires lawful, proportionate handling of personal information, and discovery analytics should follow purpose limitation and data minimization rather than collecting every available signal (Republic of the Philippines 2012; National Privacy Commission 2023). A useful dashboard does not justify indefinite storage of identifiable queries.

Professional judgment prompt: A vendor offers “better personalization” if the library sends persistent user identifiers and complete search histories. What evidence would justify the benefit, what data could be avoided, how long would records be retained, and what nonpersonalized option would remain?

6.8 Review questions

  1. Identification: What is an inverted index, and how does it differ from a list of words stored inside one document record?
  2. Identification: Define precision and recall. What does the denominator of each measure represent?
  3. Short answer: Why can a relevant item fail to appear even when the user chooses reasonable search terms? Name three different layers.
  4. Short answer: Explain why full-text indexing does not eliminate the need for controlled metadata and authority control.
  5. Applied search: Translate the need “recent Filipino-language materials for parents about children’s online privacy” into a concept map and a sample Boolean query.
  6. Applied analysis: A search retrieves 12 items, 9 of which are relevant. Evaluators know of 15 relevant items in the corpus. Calculate precision and recall and interpret both values.
  7. Scenario: A repository record appears in the local repository but not in the university discovery layer. What evidence would you collect before changing the ranking configuration?
  8. Scenario: A facet lists Thesis, Dissertation, Text, PDF, and Electronic resource under one heading. Diagnose the conceptual problem and propose a clearer design.
  9. Scenario: The top result links to a journal page, but the user’s institution has access through another provider. Which services or data should be checked?
  10. Critical analysis: How might usage-based ranking reproduce existing inequalities in a regional cultural heritage portal? Propose two safeguards.

6.9 Mini case: The missing coastal knowledge collection

Baybay State College launches a discovery layer linking its catalogue, institutional repository, and selected databases. Faculty search for locally produced work on mangrove rehabilitation. The repository contains 46 theses and extension reports, but only 11 appear in discovery. Searching the municipality’s current name finds few older studies. Searching mangrove ranks international articles above local reports. Several local records have titles and dates but no subjects, abstracts, geographic fields, or stable links. The vendor recommends boosting the repository domain globally. The university archivist warns that some reports contain names and precise locations supplied by vulnerable coastal households.

6.9.1 Guide questions

  1. Which coverage, metadata, indexing, ranking, access, privacy, and governance problems can you identify? Keep causes separate from symptoms.
  2. What test set would you build to determine whether historical place names, local terminology, and repository records are retrievable?
  3. What phased improvement would balance local visibility with privacy and avoid making every repository item rank highly regardless of relevance?

6.10 Further reading and tools

6.10.1 Readings and standards

  • Manning, Raghavan, and Schuetze, Introduction to Information Retrieval. A freely available foundational text on indexing, Boolean retrieval, ranking, and evaluation (Manning et al. 2008).
  • Robertson and Zaragoza, “The Probabilistic Relevance Framework.” A deeper explanation of BM25 and its assumptions (Robertson and Zaragoza 2009).
  • NISO Open Discovery Initiative. Recommended practice on transparency and exchange among content and discovery providers (National Information Standards Organization 2020).
  • ANSI/NISO Z39.88, OpenURL. The framework underlying many context-sensitive linking services (The OpenURL Framework for Context-Sensitive Services 2010).
  • Library of Congress Z39.50 Maintenance Agency. Background and documentation for a long-standing search-and-retrieval standard (Library of Congress 2026).
  • Open Archives Initiative Protocol for Metadata Harvesting. Essential for understanding repository metadata aggregation (Open Archives Initiative 2008).
  • Walters on recall, precision, and relevance in library assessment. A bridge from IR measures to collection and service evaluation (Walters 2016).

6.10.2 Open or widely accessible tools

  • Apache Solr and Elasticsearch/OpenSearch: search platforms for experimenting with fields, analyzers, facets, and ranking. Begin with a small non-sensitive dataset.
  • Apache Lucene documentation: technical reference for the search library beneath many systems, including BM25 scoring (Apache Lucene Project 2025).
  • VuFind: an open-source library resource discovery interface useful for examining records, facets, and integrations.
  • Blacklight: an open-source discovery framework commonly used with Solr.
  • OpenRefine: useful for inspecting and reconciling metadata values before indexing.
  • R and Quarto: appropriate for reproducible search logs, relevance calculations, tables, and reports. The calculations in this chapter use base R so that no additional package is required (R Core Team 2026; Posit 2026).

6.10.3 Closing synthesis

Information retrieval is a chain of representations and decisions. Users represent needs as queries; institutions represent resources as records and indexes; systems represent relevance as scores; interfaces represent choices as ranked lists and facets. At every stage, something can be clarified, lost, privileged, or hidden.

For LIS professionals, the practical question is not simply “Does search work?” It is: Which users can discover which resources, through which language and metadata, under what ranking and access rules, with what evidence of success, and with what consequences for privacy and equity? Chapter 7 builds on this question by introducing programming as a way to inspect, transform, and test structured information work reproducibly.
